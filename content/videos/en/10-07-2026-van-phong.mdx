At 8am, the alarm rings for an online course he'd signed up for the week before. He turns it off, tells himself five more minutes. When he opens his eyes again, the sun is already high — 11am.

![Moment 1](/characters/10-07-2026-van-phong-1.png)

He turns the alarm off for good this time, no snooze. Not laziness — his body spoke up before his mind could argue. The course textbook stays untouched on the desk.

![Moment 2](/characters/10-07-2026-van-phong-2.png)

That afternoon, a different book — the one he meant to read — meets the same fate. He plans to get through a few chapters, but his hand opens a movie instead. He doesn't remember when it ended, only that his eyes open again at 5pm.

![Moment 3](/characters/10-07-2026-van-phong-3.png)

By evening, he opens the to-do app out of habit. A long row of empty boxes. He scrolls back through last week: Monday's meetings from 8 to 6, Tuesday's overtime until 9, Wednesday's 8pm call with the overseas team, Thursday's deadline, Friday's review for the whole team. He orders dinner, closes the app for good, and doesn't open it again.

> An empty list isn't laziness.
> It's a week that never stopped.

An empty to-do doesn't mean the day meant nothing. If your last week looked anything like Minh's, does a day of doing nothing really deserve blame?
