She studies far from home and only comes back on holidays. It's 8pm, and she's dragging her
suitcase down a dark hallway, the wheels rattling on concrete. A thin line of warm light
under the door — someone is still up. Before she can knock, she looks down at her feet.

![Moment 1](/characters/12-07-2026-chung-1.png)

The plastic slippers are already there. No note taped to the door, no text on her phone.
Just the old pair she's worn since high school, the ones her mother never threw out. Her
mom never asked what time she'd arrive. She only knew her daughter was coming home today,
and at some point she set them out.

![Moment 2](/characters/12-07-2026-chung-2.png)

She bends down and slips them on. They still fit, as if she'd never left. From the kitchen
comes the clatter of a pot and the smell of rice drifting all the way to the door. Her mom
doesn't come out to greet her — she's busy turning the food, tasting the soup again to get
it right. Her daughter stands there, eyes stinging a little, not sure whether it's the
steam or something else.

![Moment 3](/characters/12-07-2026-chung-3.png)

Then she notices the small thing: the slippers are turned inward. Not tossed down at random.
Her mother had swiveled them around, so her daughter could step straight in without bending
to fix them. A three-second gesture, done before she arrived, and never mentioned.

> She never says she misses me.
> She just turns the slippers around.

She sets the suitcase down and stands in the doorway a moment longer, in no rush to go in.
Some people love you by saying nothing at all. In your house — where does that thing live?
