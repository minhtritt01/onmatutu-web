9am, Nam's desk phone won't stop ringing. On the other end is a client, voice sharp, reporting the system crashed right during peak hours. Nam pulls up the logs, checks line by line — the error came from the client's own misconfiguration, not from anything he wrote.

---

Nam explains clearly, sends screenshots, walks through exactly where it went wrong. The client isn't having it, hangs up, then fires off a complaint straight to Nam's manager. Fifteen minutes later, his manager calls him into a private room and shuts the door.

---

Nam lays it all out again: system logs, screenshots of the email, a full timeline down to the minute — enough to prove the error wasn't his. His manager listens to all of it, nods, doesn't push back on a single point. But the last line is still: "Just go ahead and fix it for the client, we're in a service business." Nam is quiet for a second, looks at his laptop screen, then nods and takes it.

---

Nam doesn't argue. He walks back to his desk, opens his laptop, and starts fixing a bug he never caused. No venting on his story. No messaging coworkers to complain. He just sits down, writes the code, tests it, closes the ticket. At exactly 6pm, he shuts his laptop and heads home — because the work still needs finishing, and whether he was wronged or not isn't what decides whether he does it.

***"Doing the right thing isn't about being seen. It's about knowing who you are."***

**When was the last time you quietly finished something, even knowing you'd been misunderstood?**
