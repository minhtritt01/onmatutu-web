6 a.m. Saturday, An's alarm goes off right on schedule, set the night before: an online course at 8, finish the half-read book in the afternoon, gym in the evening. An turns it off, mutters "five more minutes." Eleven a.m. is the next time An opens her eyes. No course, no book — just lying there staring at the ceiling, then putting on a show and watching straight through to 3 p.m. Another nap until 5. That night, food delivery, eaten right there in bed, no bother clearing a table.

---

At 11 a.m., the moment she wakes up the second time, the guilt shows up on its own. *"Why are you still lying here? Other people stay productive on weekends."* The line loops through the afternoon — while watching the show, while eating. Her phone still shows the same three-item to-do list, not one line crossed off.

---

An replays the week that just ended: Monday, meetings from 8 a.m. to 6 p.m. Tuesday, overtime until 9 p.m. Wednesday, an 8 p.m. call with a team in another time zone. Thursday, a deadline so tight there was no time for lunch. Friday, reviewing the whole team's work until almost midnight. Five straight days without a single gap that belonged to her.

An looks up at the ceiling and thinks: if a phone runs out of battery, you charge it — nobody calls that unreasonable, nobody tells the phone to "push a little harder." A person needs charging the same way. Today is that day.

---

That night, An closes the to-do list — for the first time, without a single word of self-blame attached.

***"Resting isn't losing. It's charging the battery so you can keep going."***

**When was the last time you let yourself rest without feeling guilty about it?**
