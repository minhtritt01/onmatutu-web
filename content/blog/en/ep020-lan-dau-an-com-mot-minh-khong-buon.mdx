7pm on a Thursday, Quang stood in the kitchen of his rented room, stir-frying a plate of vegetables and frying two eggs. Nothing special — just an ordinary dinner for someone who's lived alone for over two years now, at 26.

---

This used to be the scene Quang avoided. Eating alone made the empty kitchen feel even emptier, so the usual move was to eat out just to be around people, or order delivery and eat in front of a phone screen — flipping between apps just to avoid looking straight at the silence of the room. Some nights he'd scroll through the news while eating and finish without remembering what he'd just had.

---

Tonight was different. Quang brought his plate out and sat in front of the TV, put on some show that didn't need much attention, just for the sound of voices. The rice wasn't perfect — a little too salty from seasoning it by feel — but it was fine. After finishing, washing his hands, he realized he hadn't felt sad or empty once during the whole meal. It was just an ordinary dinner, like any other, from someone who's grown used to living alone.

---

Quang sat for a few more minutes on the chair, looking around the small room. A meal doesn't need someone sitting across from you to feel complete. Maybe this is the point where you start getting used to yourself — not because the loneliness is gone, but because you no longer need to fill it with someone else's noise.

***"You're getting used to yourself now. That's its own kind of growing up."***

**Have you ever eaten alone and felt fine, instead of feeling like something was missing?**
